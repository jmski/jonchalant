## The load-bearing argument (goes on the Foundation page)

This is the paragraph that has to do the heaviest lifting on the site. It earns the transfer claim — the idea that embodiment training through dance applies to any medium. Without this, skeptical professionals bounce.

> **Why dance?**
>
> Because embodiment is hard to learn in front of a spreadsheet. Dance forces it. You can't fake being grounded when you're about to jump. You can't intellectualise rhythm. Every shortcut shows up in your body, immediately, in front of other people — and that feedback loop is what makes it the fastest teacher of presence I've ever found.
>
> Once you've felt what grounded presence is in your body — learned it, not read about it — you can recognise it and reproduce it anywhere. At a podium. On a sales call. In a difficult conversation with your team. Walking into a room where you don't feel like you belong.
>
> The medium changes. The fundamentals don't. What you do with what you learn is up to you.

## The four pillars — reframed as medium-agnostic

Use these on the home page pillar grid, and as section framing on the Foundation page. The structure: pillar name → one-line definition → three mini-applications across different mediums. This is the single strongest piece of proof that embodiment transfers.

### Grounding

**The capacity to find your centre before you move.**

- A dancer finds it before the first step. Without it, every movement after is compensating.
- A leader finds it before they speak. Without it, the room reads the uncertainty before the words land.
- A writer finds it before the first sentence. Without it, the page fights back.

### Energy

**Calibrating your presence to the room.**

- A performer reads the audience and adjusts — not performing harder, performing precisely.
- A salesperson reads the client and adjusts — not louder, more matched.
- Anyone managing their own output learns to spend energy where it moves the work, not where it feels busy.

### Flow

**Moving between rigid structure and live response.**

- Dancers drill choreography until they can forget it. Then they can actually dance it.
- Leaders prepare the message until they can leave it. Then they can actually have the conversation.
- Professionals in any medium learn when to follow the plan and when to abandon it — in real time, without panicking.

### Command

**Presence that carries authority without force.**

- Quiet command on stage. The dancer who doesn't have to chase attention.
- Quiet command in a meeting. The person who speaks less and shifts the room more.
- Quiet command in your own life. Deciding what you're here for, and behaving like someone who knows.
